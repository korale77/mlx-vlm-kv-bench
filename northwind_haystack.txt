NORTHWIND STATION — Engineering Handbook, Edition 7

This document describes the architecture, operations, and maintenance procedures
for Northwind Station, a synthetic subsea research platform operating at a
nominal depth of 412 meters on the Telkwa Rise. The station is owned and
operated by the fictional Cascadia Deep Research Institute and exists solely
as reference material for engineering training exercises. Any resemblance to
real installations is coincidental.

1. MISSION PROFILE

Northwind Station supports three primary workloads: long-duration benthic
observation (optical, acoustic, and chemical sensors), fluid sampling for
hydrothermal vents, and periodic ROV launch and recovery. The station
operates on a 90-day rotation cycle with a permanent crew of 14, divided into
three watches. A fourth watch rotates in during surface supply windows, which
occur every 30 days weather permitting. Northwind has operated continuously
since its commissioning in March 2019.

2. POWER SYSTEMS

Primary power is supplied by a 1.8 megawatt thorium-fluoride reactor housed
in the aft pressure module. The reactor is thermally coupled to a closed
Brayton-cycle turbine spinning at 34,000 rpm. Electrical output is nominally
1.6 megawatts at 480 volts three-phase, with 200 kilowatts reserved as
spinning margin. A secondary array of zinc-bromide flow batteries provides
48 hours of hotel load at a 40 percent duty cycle if the reactor is offline.
Emergency tertiary power comes from a 240 kilowatt hydrogen fuel cell stack
stored in the forward safety vestibule, good for approximately six hours at
full station load.

Switching between the primary, secondary, and tertiary sources is managed by
the Power Allocation Controller (PAC), a triple-redundant fault-tolerant
computer running the RTEMS real-time operating system. Transitions must
complete within 200 milliseconds to avoid brownouts on the life support
busbars. The PAC logs every transition to non-volatile memory and replicates
the logs to the surface via the monthly data upload.

3. LIFE SUPPORT

The atmospheric control system maintains the crew habitat at a pressure of
1.05 atmospheres, 21.0 percent oxygen, and 78.5 percent nitrogen with trace
argon. Carbon dioxide is scrubbed by a two-stage molecular sieve bed plus a
backup lithium hydroxide cartridge tray. Humidity is held between 45 and 55
percent relative, with excess condensate fed back into the potable water
reclamation loop. The crew consumes an average of 2.4 kilograms of oxygen
per person per day; the station stockpiles a 60-day margin of cryogenic
liquid oxygen in tank LOX-3.

Water is reclaimed from condensate, urine, and sink graywater through a
four-stage filtration train: particulate, activated carbon, reverse osmosis,
and ultraviolet polishing. The system recovers 92 percent of input water
after the UV stage. Potable output is tested hourly for total dissolved
solids and microbial load by the Automated Water Quality Monitor installed
at junction WQM-A7.

Thermal control is handled by three cooling loops. Loop A rejects reactor
waste heat into the surrounding ocean through a titanium heat exchanger on
the aft pressure module. Loop B services the electronics bay and is
temperature-limited to 18 degrees Celsius by a set of proportional valves
controlled by the Habitat Climate Node. Loop C is a dedicated medical and
food-storage loop maintained at 4 degrees Celsius.

4. COMMUNICATIONS

Surface-to-station communications rely on an acoustic modem at 10 kilohertz
with a maximum throughput of 18 kilobits per second. The modem is backed up
by a tethered fiber-optic umbilical that provides 1 gigabit per second when
the surface tender is on-station. An emergency very-low-frequency radio
beacon transmits a four-character status code every hour on the hour at
2.4 kilohertz; receipt of this beacon is required before the surface tender
will authorize any crew extraction.

Inside the habitat, crew carry ultrasonic ID tags that broadcast at 42 kHz
once per second. The Habitat Mesh Network logs each tag's position to the
nearest 40 centimeters and uses the data to automatically adjust ventilation
to occupied spaces. Tags must be recharged every 72 hours in the tag cradle
at station K-2 near the main airlock.

5. SAFETY AND EMERGENCY PROCEDURES

In the event of a hull breach, the Emergency Compartmentalization System
(ECS) seals the affected module within 4.2 seconds using explosive bolts to
release redundant bulkhead doors. Crew in the affected compartment have
approximately 90 seconds to exit via the nearest escape hatch before the
doors lock for the remainder of the incident. All crew members wear
rebreather collars that automatically inflate on exposure to seawater and
provide 40 minutes of oxygen.

Fire response follows the Inert Gas Discharge Protocol. Argon is flooded
into the affected compartment to reduce oxygen to 12 percent, starving the
fire while remaining survivable for crew wearing emergency breathing masks.
The argon supply is sized for two full compartment floods per 90-day cycle.

The primary escape vehicle is ESP-1, a six-person buoyant pod rated for
surface ascent from 500 meters. Ascent time is 7 minutes from release to
surface, with an internal pressure equalization program that prevents
decompression injury. A secondary pod, ESP-2, carries four crew and is
rated for 450 meters.

6. MAINTENANCE CALENDAR

Routine maintenance follows the 30/90/365 schedule.

30-day items:
- Replace CO2 scrubber cartridge bank A.
- Rotate the water quality reference sensors against the backup calibration
  standards in lab L-3.
- Perform a dry run of the fire suppression system without discharging argon.
- Rinse the external acoustic modem transducer head with isopropyl alcohol
  and inspect for biofouling.
- Verify all rebreather collars are within expiration and have a full charge.

90-day items:
- Swap the reactor primary coolant filter.
- Calibrate the Power Allocation Controller using the reference load bank.
- Perform a full drill of the Emergency Compartmentalization System on one
  rotating compartment per cycle.
- Inspect the titanium heat exchanger for biofouling and sacrificial anode
  wear. Replace anodes if greater than 40 percent consumed.
- Replenish the argon reservoir to full capacity during the surface supply
  window.

365-day items:
- Full reactor core inspection by a certified inspection team deployed from
  the surface tender.
- Replacement of all Brayton-cycle turbine bearings.
- End-to-end communications blackout drill.
- Audit of every crew medical kit and replacement of any item within 60
  days of expiration.
- Dry dock inspection of ESP-1 and ESP-2, including pressure-testing the
  hull to 110 percent of rated depth.

8. SCIENTIFIC INSTRUMENTATION

The station's primary observation package is the OCEANUS-7 optical array,
consisting of 14 high-sensitivity cameras and 6 fiber-optic hydrophones.
OCEANUS-7 produces approximately 2.3 terabytes of raw data per day, which
is compressed using a custom codec to approximately 140 gigabytes before
being staged for monthly uplink. The compression is lossy for the camera
frames but lossless for the hydrophone streams.

Secondary instruments include a 50-channel mass spectrometer for dissolved
gas analysis, a pair of benthic manipulator arms nicknamed Sagan and Hypatia,
and a fluid sampling carousel capable of holding 48 independent samples at
in-situ pressure. The carousel rotates once per sample collection and has a
minimum cycle time of 90 seconds between samples.

9. DATA RETENTION POLICY

Raw sensor data from OCEANUS-7 is retained on the station for 30 days, then
overwritten in ring-buffer fashion. Compressed data is retained for 18 months
and archived to the Institute's long-term store at the surface facility.
Engineering telemetry, including all Power Allocation Controller logs, is
retained indefinitely and is never overwritten. The retention policy is
enforced by the Data Lifecycle Manager, a small service running on the
station's primary compute cluster.

The compute cluster itself consists of 12 Apple silicon nodes arranged in a
passively cooled chassis. Cluster nominal compute is 48 teraflops fp16.
Crew members requesting compute for ad hoc analysis must submit a request
through the Cluster Access Portal at least two hours before their intended
start time.
